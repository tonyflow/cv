### Leadership style
1. This depends on the size of the project and it does not apply to smaller projects but for medium sized projects and above or for projects which span across multiple systems one has to make sure that there are clean interfaces in the design. This has the benefit that once defined, the leading engineer can bring the right people with the corresponding know-how into the project. If there are no clean intetfaces and the project is sizeable I prefer keeping the amount of people limited - one supervising engineer and one leading engineer. I am a supporter of that since I've seen that resolving conflicts on the merging phase can take more time than keeping the effort contained bewteen these two people.
2. Make sure that there's a clear understanding of what the business/product requirement and what the added value would be. 
   a. We're working in the industry and eventually new features are driven by business - whether it comes from product or engineering. Having a clear understanding of what we're trying to achieve is of paramount importance in constructing a clear vision of how we are going to achieve that.
   b. Understanding the added value will drive the testing of the system. Does my implementation/design achieves what was initially designed to do? How am I going to test this more rigorously?
3. Guide the engineer in creating a comprehensive design doc - even if it's a small one. This allows the engineer to articulate one's thoughts in written word. By doing so one understands flaws in ones reasoning and can correct one's course. This design doc might need to go through one or two iterations. It should reach a state where the engineer is able to 
   a. Create tasks out of this this. The tasks should span the entire lifecycle of the project
   b. The tasks should represent small incremental and deployable iterations of the project. I usually like to abide to the RUP as a point of reference regarding to how the work should be structured.
Throughout the design doc writing there are different levels of consulting and I will always be there for the engineer whether this is on a technical level or a business level. 
3.1. The design document should provide a clean vision of the project
4. I consider my responsibility as a supervising engineer to teach a more junior member of the team how to interact with the other teams. In the end leading is not about knowing everything but it's mostly about employing all the resources at your disposal in an efficient way to reach a successful result.
5. Maybe a presentation to the team is required
6. The engineer creates the task. Having designed the feature and planned one's own work is a great boost for one's sense of ownership. I have experienced this myself and I deliberately avoid creating any tasks - apart from exceptional cases - for someone else's projects.
7. All PRs should go through two reviews: One from the supervising engineer and one from another engineer in order to spread the know how and not stay in silos. At least one review is necessary.
8. Small retrospective

On a human side:
- Self-leadership
- Time management
- Try to be more open,receptive and undestanding as possible. Especially if you do not know the other person
- Try to talk as less as possible
- Try to listen as much as possible
- Also be skeptical about the way you communicate things since we're all different and there's no one way of communication that fits all of us.
